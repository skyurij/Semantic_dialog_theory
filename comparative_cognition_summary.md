# Comparative Cognition Summary  
### Three Cognitive Types and Their Meaning‑Dynamic Trajectories  
*(Supplementary material for “Stable Motion in Layered Phase Spaces and an Invariant of Meaning Dynamics”)*

This document summarizes the three instructional dialogues (Iosif, Gosha, Benya)  
and compares their cognitive types, semantic‑dynamic trajectories, and synchrony patterns  
within the layered phase‑space model developed in the main paper.

Each student represents a distinct cognitive regime, producing a distinct form of motion  
in the semantic phase space.

---

# 1. Overview Table

| Student | Cognitive Type | Dominant Mode | Phase‑Space Motion | Synchrony Level |
|--------|----------------|---------------|--------------------|-----------------|
| **Iosif** | Structural–Intuitive | Internal visual models, holistic patterns | Non‑linear, jumps between layers | **65–70%** |
| **Gosha** | Kinesthetic–Constructive | Action‑based reasoning, manipulation | Oscillatory, unstable under abstraction | **40–45%** |
| **Benya** | Analytic–Constructive | Logical chains, abstraction, precision | Stable, monotonic, invariant‑preserving | **80–85%** |

---

# 2. Cognitive Type Profiles

## 2.1 Iosif — Structural–Intuitive Thinker  
- Thinks in **internal visual structures** (“brackets”, “blocks”).  
- Does not anchor symbolic notation (x, y, fractions).  
- Makes **non‑linear jumps** in reasoning.  
- Sees **global patterns** before local steps.  
- Insight arises when structure becomes visually coherent.

**Phase‑space interpretation:**  
Iosif moves along **curved, non‑monotonic trajectories** between layers.  
He preserves structure only when the teacher aligns with his internal model.

---

## 2.2 Gosha — Kinesthetic–Constructive Thinker  
- Understands through **physical action** (folding, touching, rotating).  
- Cannot maintain abstraction without motor support.  
- Loses multi‑step reasoning after 1–2 steps.  
- Insight arises through **action**, not visualization or logic.

**Phase‑space interpretation:**  
Gosha’s trajectory is **oscillatory**:  
stable when grounded in action, unstable when forced into abstraction.  
He frequently “falls out of phase” when the teacher moves to higher layers.

---

## 2.3 Benya — Analytic–Constructive Thinker  
- Maintains **abstract structure** effortlessly.  
- Holds multiple constraints simultaneously.  
- Provides precise logical justification.  
- Corrects hypotheses quickly and cleanly.  
- Insight arises through **logical condensation**.

**Phase‑space interpretation:**  
Benya exhibits **stable, monotonic motion** within a single layer.  
He preserves invariants and maintains structural coherence throughout.

---

# 3. Meaning‑Dynamic Trajectories

## 3.1 Iosif — Non‑Linear Structural Trajectory  
- Moves between layers unpredictably.  
- Synchrony increases when the teacher adopts his visual model.  
- Synchrony collapses when symbolic formalism is introduced.  
- Exhibits **invariant sensitivity** but not symbolic stability.

This corresponds to **quasi‑periodic motion** with intermittent locking.

---

## 3.2 Gosha — Action‑Driven Oscillatory Trajectory  
- Stable only in the “action layer”.  
- Abstraction forces him into desynchronization.  
- Insight occurs only when action is reintroduced.  
- Cognitive motion resembles **forced oscillation** with phase slips.

This is the least stable trajectory of the three.

---

## 3.3 Benya — Stable Analytic Trajectory  
- Maintains a single cognitive layer consistently.  
- Preserves structural invariants (common measure, parity, constraints).  
- Synchrony with the teacher is high and persistent.  
- Cognitive motion is **monotonic and invariant‑preserving**.

This is the most stable trajectory.

---

# 4. Invariant Ratio and Cognitive Types

The invariant ratio \( R = \frac{p}{T} \)  
(structural density over topological freedom)  
behaves differently for each student.

| Student | Structural Density \(p\) | Topological Freedom \(T\) | Invariant Behavior |
|--------|---------------------------|----------------------------|--------------------|
| **Iosif** | Medium | Medium–High | Fluctuating, intermittent |
| **Gosha** | Low | High | Unstable, drifting |
| **Benya** | High | Medium | Approximately constant |

Interpretation:

- **Iosif**: invariant intermittently preserved → quasi‑stable motion  
- **Gosha**: invariant breaks frequently → unstable motion  
- **Benya**: invariant preserved → stable motion

This aligns with the stable‑motion theorem in the main paper.

---

# 5. Semantic Synchrony Patterns

## Iosif  
Synchrony rises when structure is visual.  
Falls when reasoning becomes symbolic.

## Gosha  
Synchrony rises when action is allowed.  
Falls when abstraction is required.

## Benya  
Synchrony remains high across all modes.  
He adapts to abstraction and structure equally well.

---

# 6. Implications for the Theory

The three dialogues provide **empirical illustrations** of the theoretical framework:

- **Layered phase spaces** correspond to cognitive modes (action, visual structure, abstraction).  
- **Stable motion** corresponds to analytic–constructive cognition (Benya).  
- **Quasi‑periodic motion** corresponds to structural–intuitive cognition (Iosif).  
- **Unstable oscillatory motion** corresponds to kinesthetic–constructive cognition (Gosha).  
- **Invariant ratio** is preserved only in the stable analytic regime.

Thus, the three students form a **natural experimental triad**  
demonstrating the core claims of the manuscript.
