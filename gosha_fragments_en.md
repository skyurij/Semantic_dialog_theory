# Key Transcript Fragments: Lesson with Gosha  
*(English translation)*

These excerpts include only the parts of the lesson that are directly referenced in the cognitive analysis.  
They illustrate Gosha’s kinesthetic–constructive thinking style, his reliance on physical action,  
and the points of semantic synchrony and desynchrony with the teacher.

---

## 1. Reliance on physical action

**Gosha:**  
“Can I take a sheet of paper?”  
“It’s easier to work with my hands.”

**Teacher:**  
“Let’s try to imagine it first.”

**Gosha:**  
“No, it’s easier with hands.”

This fragment shows that Gosha cannot maintain an abstract model without physical manipulation.

---

## 2. Difficulty holding multi‑step abstractions

**Teacher:**  
“First we fold along this line, then along this one, then along the next…”

**Gosha:**  
“Wait… I don’t get it.”

This illustrates the breakdown when the teacher moves into multi‑step abstract reasoning.

---

## 3. Moment of insight through action

**Gosha:**  
“Oh! I get it — we can fold it like this!”  
(He describes a physical folding sequence.)

This is a classic kinesthetic insight: understanding emerges only when he imagines a physical action.

---

## 4. Visual–concrete reasoning

**Gosha:**  
“It can’t be A, because the dots are separated.”  
“If there’s a gap between the dots, it won’t work.”

This shows his ability to reason visually when the structure is concrete.

---

## 5. Breakdown when abstraction increases

**Teacher:**  
“Try to imagine the layers aligning.”

**Gosha:**  
“I don’t understand… maybe… I don’t know.”

This fragment is used in the analysis to show the drop in synchrony when the teacher moves to abstraction.

---

## 6. Meta‑comment revealing his cognitive style

**Gosha:**  
“You sometimes use the same words, but I don’t understand.”

This is a direct indicator of his need for concrete, action‑based explanation.

---

## Summary

These fragments illustrate:

- Gosha’s kinesthetic–constructive cognitive type  
- His dependence on physical manipulation  
- His difficulty with multi‑step abstract reasoning  
- His strong visual reasoning when the task is concrete  
- The points where semantic synchrony rises or collapses

They correspond directly to the analysis in `gosha_analysis_en.md`.
